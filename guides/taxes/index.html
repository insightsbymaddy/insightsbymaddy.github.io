TAX TERM #1

INCOME
TAX

Íos-Cháin Ioncaim — Republic of Ireland only

Income Tax is the government's cut of everything you earn. Ireland uses a two-rate system: a standard rate of 20% on income up to a threshold, and a higher rate of 40% on everything above it. The threshold depends on your circumstances — single, married, or with a qualifying child.

Personal Circumstances (2026)Standard Rate BandAbove the Band
Single person 20% on first €44,000 40% on balance
Single parent (SPCCC) 20% on first €48,000 40% on balance
Married / Civil partnership (one income) 20% on first €53,000 40% on balance
Married / Civil partnership (both earning) 20% on first €53,000 + up to €35,000 more 40% on balance
Important: The higher rate band increase for married couples is capped at the lower of €35,000 or the lower earner's income — and it cannot be transferred between spouses.

TAX TERM #2

USC
UNIVERSAL
SOCIAL CHARGE

Muirear Sóisialta Uilíoch — Republic of Ireland only

Introduced in 2011 as a temporary emergency measure. It's still here in 2026. USC is effectively a second income tax applied in bands on top of your Income Tax. If you earn more than €13,000 in a year, you pay USC on your full income — not just the amount above €13,000.

Income Band (2026)Cumulative IncomeRate
First €12,012 €0 → €12,012 0.5%
Next €16,688 €12,012 → €28,700 2%
Next €41,344 €28,700 → €70,044 3%
Balance above €70,044 €70,044+ 8%
Watch out: If your income is over €13,000, you pay USC on your full income from €0. There's no tax-free threshold — once you cross the line, USC applies to every euro you earned.

TAX TERM #3

PRSI
PAY RELATED
SOCIAL INSURANCE

Árachas Sóisialta Pá-Choibhneasa — Republic of Ireland only

PRSI is your contribution into the national Social Insurance Fund. Think of it as paying into a pot. In return, you build up entitlements — Jobseeker's Benefit, Maternity Benefit, Illness Benefit, and eventually the State Pension. Most employees pay Class A PRSI at 4.2% of gross pay (this rate increased from 4.1% on 1 October 2025). Your employer also pays PRSI on your behalf — typically at 11.15%.

Contributor TypeClassRate (from Oct 2025)
Employee (most workers) Class A 4.2% of gross pay
Employer contribution Class A 11.15% of gross pay
Self-employed Class S 4.2% of gross income
Unearned income (rents, dividends, savings interest) if over €5,000 Class K 4.2% — does NOT build benefits
Benefits you build up: Jobseeker's Benefit, Maternity Benefit (€289/week in 2026), Paternity Benefit, Illness Benefit, Carer's Benefit, and the State Pension (Contributory). You need a minimum number of "stamps" (contributions) to qualify for each benefit.

TAX TERM #4 — THE GOOD NEWS

TAX
CREDITS

Creidmheasanna Cánach — Republic of Ireland only

Tax credits are the best thing about the Irish tax system. Unlike a tax relief (which reduces your taxable income), a credit directly reduces the actual tax you owe. Every euro of credit saves you one euro of tax. As a PAYE employee, you automatically get two credits worth €4,000 per year — no claiming required.

Home Carer Credit (max) Married couples where one partner cares at home €1,950

TAX TERM #5 — WHEN YOU SELL

CGT
CAPITAL
GAINS TAX

Cáin Gnóthachan Caipitiúil — Republic of Ireland only

Capital Gains Tax applies when you sell (or gift, or exchange) an asset for more than you paid for it. The taxable amount is the profit (the gain), not the full sale price. The current CGT rate in Ireland is 33% on most gains. You get a personal annual exemption of €1,270 — gains below this are tax-free.

CGT applies to: shares, property (not your primary home), crypto assets, land, business assets, and most other valuable things you sell for a profit.

33%
CGT Rate
Applies on most capital gains including shares, crypto, and investment property.
€1,270
Annual Exemption
Your first €1,270 of gains each year are tax-free. This cannot be carried over or transferred.
0%
Primary Home (PPR)
CGT does not apply to the sale of your principal private residence (with conditions).

Payment deadlines — critical: Gains from January to November must be paid by 15 December of the same year. Gains made in December must be paid by 31 January of the following year. Missing these dates results in interest charges.
Losses can offset gains: If you made a loss on one investment and a gain on another in the same year, you can offset the loss against the gain to reduce your CGT bill. Unused losses can also be carried forward to future tax years.
Official source: Revenue.ie — Capital Gains Tax on the disposal of an asset

TAX TERM #6 — THE ONE NOBODY TELLS YOU

DEEMED
DISPOSAL

Diúscairt Mheasúnaithe — Republic of Ireland only · Unique to Ireland

Deemed Disposal is a rule unique to Ireland that applies to ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds) and other investment funds. Under Section 739G of the Taxes Consolidation Act 1997, Revenue treats you as if you sold your investment every 8 years — even if you didn't. Any gain at that point is taxable at 38% (reduced from 41% in January 2026).

This means you can owe real money in tax on paper gains you haven't realised — gains that exist only on paper and could disappear tomorrow. You must find cash to pay the tax on profits you haven't pocketed.

38%
Tax Rate
Reduced from 41% in January 2026. Applies to gains on ETFs and investment funds held in Irish or EU domiciled structures.
8 yrs
Trigger Point
Revenue deems you to have sold on the 8th anniversary of your investment — and every 8 years after that.
€0
Annual Exemption
Unlike CGT, there is no €1,270 annual exemption. The full gain is taxable at 38%.

Real-world consequence: You must pay €3,040 in cash to Revenue — even though you still own the ETF and haven't sold anything. If your ETF value drops after the deemed disposal date, you can't recover the tax already paid. This is why many Irish investors face a significant disadvantage compared to investors in the UK, US, or elsewhere in Europe.
Does not apply to: Individual shares (covered by CGT at 33%), directly held property, or pension funds. It only applies to ETFs, investment funds, and life assurance investment products that fall under the "exit tax" regime for funds. Always verify with a tax professional whether your specific product is caught by this rule.
Official source: Revenue.ie — Investment Undertakings, Section 739G TCA 1997
TAX TERM #7 — LEAVING IRELAND?

EXIT
TAX

Cáin Imeachta — Republic of Ireland only

Exit Tax (also called a departure tax or "clawback") applies in two distinct contexts in Ireland:

1. Emigrating from Ireland: If you cease to be Irish tax resident while holding investment assets (including shares and funds), Revenue may tax any gains that accrued while you were a resident — before you leave. This prevents people from moving abroad, selling there, and avoiding Irish tax entirely.

2. Fund Exit Tax (on investment products): When you actually sell or exit an Irish-domiciled investment fund or life assurance product, a tax of 38% applies to any gain. This is sometimes also called "exit tax" and is closely related to the Deemed Disposal regime.

38%
Rate on Fund Exits
Applies when you actually sell out of an Irish-domiciled investment fund or life product.
33%
Rate on Other Assets
Gains on shares, crypto, and property when you emigrate are subject to standard CGT at 33%.
4 yrs
Look-back Period
Certain assets may be brought back into the Irish tax net if you return within 5 years of emigrating.

TAX TERM #8 — YOUR SAVINGS ACCOUNT

DIRT
DEPOSIT INTEREST
RETENTION TAX

Cáin Choinneála ar Ús Taisce — Republic of Ireland only

DIRT is the tax on interest earned in Irish savings accounts, deposit accounts, and credit union accounts. Your bank or financial institution deducts it automatically — you never see the full interest in your account. The DIRT rate has been 33% since 2020 and remains at 33% for 2026.

Most people never notice DIRT because it's silent. The interest displayed in your online banking is already after DIRT has been taken.

33%
DIRT Rate (2026)
Deducted automatically by your bank before interest is credited to your account.
0%
Over 65s / Disabled
If you're aged 65+ (or permanently incapacitated) and your total income is below the exemption limit, you can reclaim DIRT or claim an exemption.
Auto
Deducted at Source
You don't file anything. The bank handles it. But it counts as a final liability — no further income tax on the same interest.

DIRT and PRSI double tax: From October 2025, if your total non-PAYE income (including savings interest) exceeds €5,000/year, you may also owe 4.2% PRSI on that interest — on top of the DIRT already paid. This is a Class K PRSI charge and does not give you any social insurance benefits.
State Savings are DIRT-free: Interest earned on State Savings products (An Post State Savings) is exempt from DIRT. These products — including savings bonds and savings certificates — can be a tax-efficient way to hold cash. See statesavings.ie for current rates.
Official source: Revenue.ie — DIRT rate confirmed at 33% for 2026
